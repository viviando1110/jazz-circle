## circle-of-fifths-jazz-guide
**Overall:** Would read again
**Hook:** Strong -- The Cannonball/Bill Evans moment of recognition is vivid and personal. "You suddenly realize the chords aren't random" is a great way to frame what the circle of fifths actually does.
**Tone:** Consistent throughout. The "classroom poster" framing is playful without being condescending. Never slips into textbook mode.
**Clarity:** Good. "Counterclockwise" is explained clearly. "Tritone" appears briefly in the Giant Steps section but isn't defined (though it was avoided elsewhere). "Contrafact" is not used, which is wise.
**Flow:** Smooth. The progression from concept to "Autumn Leaves" to "All the Things" to "Giant Steps" feels like a natural escalation. Each section builds on the last.
**Shareability:** Yes -- "The Prediction Machine" is a compelling final section. I'd send this to a guitar-playing friend who's curious about jazz.
**Top suggestion:** The opening paragraph is slightly long (5 sentences). Consider breaking after "They're pulling you somewhere" for pacing. The payoff ("That's the circle of fifths doing its thing") gets buried.

## Cross-cutting issues seen in multiple posts

- **The Clifford Brown quote is used at least 3 times** (ii-V-I, arpeggios, question-answer, and what-is-a-lead-sheet). This is the single biggest editorial problem. It makes the blog look like it was batch-produced from a template. Each post needs a unique quote, or the pullquote feature should be used more sparingly.
- **Autumn Leaves fatigue.** This tune is referenced in at least 8 of 15 posts. It's the default example for everything. While it IS the most commonly played standard, the repetition across posts makes the blog feel narrow. Vary the examples more -- use "There Will Never Be Another You," "Stella by Starlight," "Softly as in a Morning Sunrise," or other tunes that illustrate the same points.
- **Every post follows the same structure:** hook paragraph, 6-8 h2 sections, practice tip, closing reflection. This works well individually but creates a sameness across the blog. A few posts could benefit from structural variation -- a post that opens with a story instead of a thesis, or one that's structured as a FAQ, or one with a shorter, punchier format.
- **The tone is remarkably consistent**, which is both a strength and a minor weakness. It's consistently warm, knowledgeable, and encouraging. But it's also consistently the same register. None of the posts get genuinely passionate, angry, or opinionated. A blog with a point of view occasionally takes a stance. Consider adding one or two posts that are more argumentative (e.g., "Stop Practicing Licks" or "Why Most Jazz Education is Backwards").
- **"Try This" tips are uniformly good.** Every practice suggestion is concrete and actionable. This is a strength across the entire set.
- **The SEO intent is well-hidden.** These don't read like SEO content dressed up as a blog. They read like genuine articles that happen to target search terms. That's the right balance. The titles and excerpts are search-friendly without being keyword-stuffed.
